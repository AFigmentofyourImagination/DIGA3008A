The Internet, Geopolitics, and Digital Coloniality.

Elri Greyling

The case study I have chosen for this essay to analyse is the renaming of the Gulf of Mexico on Google Maps for U.S. users in the context of the internet, geopolitics, and digital coloniality. This is so that I may understand how these three concepts interact with one another in the real world.

To establish context, on the 20th of January 2025 the Gulf of Mexico was to be renamed as the Gulf of America by U.S. federal agencies. This executive order was signed by the current U.S. president. On Google Maps, the Gulf of Mexico was changed to the Gulf of America for only U.S. users whereas for the rest of the world it was still referred to as the Gulf of Mexico, although the Gulf of America was put in brackets afterwards. This has sparked a lot of controversy and even a lawsuit against Google from the Mexican president for the rename.

Many have speculated this renaming is because it is to establish power. This opinion is backed by scholars.

Williamson (2023) wrote, “Exploring the erasure of native place names demonstrates how naming or renaming in the process of colonial expansion was fundamental to the exertion of control over physical and social environments (Grounds, 2001).”

The Gulf of Mexico was named that all the way back in the early 1540’s, before that it had no name. By renaming it means to rid of all the history it has. To rename it means rewriting its history.

Google had renamed the Gulf of Mexico because it is a company based in America and unfortunately the rest of the world, especially us in the South, is reliant on their information.

“Almost all mediums of information (e.g. book publishing, newspaper publications, and patents) in the early twenty-first century are still characterized by huge geographic inequalities: with the Global North producing, consuming, and controlling much of the world’s codified knowledge, and the Global South largely left out of these processes.” (Graham, 2014: 104).

With the Global North dominant in our access to information, the renaming of the Gulf of Mexico can cause the rich history of the location to be forgotten in future generations.

“As geography becomes ever more digital, search engines thus increasingly mediate not just information but also spatial knowledges and experiences.” (Ballatore et al. 2017: 1195).

History is important so we may know where we came from and how we have changed as humans, thus rewriting it is harmful.

“Although a variety of Web search services and technologies exist, the search market is controlled by a handful of large actors.” (Ballatore et al. 2017: 1196).

This quote reinforces the notion that history is written by the conqueror, and in this case, the large actors are Google and the companies’ want to be in the U.S. government’s good graces because the U.S. has power.

In 2017, Ballatore et al. (2017, 1196) has established that Google is the dominant search engine, with 64% of U.S. residents and 88% of U.K. residents, and having 54% of the entire world’s search advertising market, which is in 188 countries.

“This monopoly has not gone unnoticed and has attracted the attention of European antitrust agencies and a variety of critics, worried about such a concentration of information, power, and capital (e.g., Vaidhyanathan 2011; Grimmelmann 2013)." (Ballatore et al. 2017: 1196).

The renaming of the Gulf of Mexico is not the first case where Google has tried to remain politically correct while sparking controversy. In 2012, Google had used the same locale-based naming to another place who were having disputes over the naming of said place. This place is the Persian Gulf. Google Maps had left the waterway between the Arabian peninsula and Iran nameless until Iran had threatened legal action against Google. Now the place reads “Persian Gulf (Arabian Gulf)”. It seems ironic that Google is caught in another renaming controversy involving another Gulf.

“We begin with the hypothesis that the Internet had a variety of effects across locations because the impact of lower communication, transportation, and search costs varies by location, depending on three main factors: local preferences, the availability of substitutes, and the availability of complements.” (Greenstein, Forman and Goldfarb, 2018: 270).

This quote explains that the Internet’s effects reach far even without access to said Internet. Google can rename any place it wants to whatever it wants because it has power over politically weaker countries and people will use Google’s name if they do not know better because people are reliant on the information and over time, the name of the place is rewritten in the image of Google, history forcibly hidden and forgotten.

“Emphasising the cultural politics of naming has revealed ‘how people seek to control, negotiate, and contest the naming process as they engage in wider struggles for legitimacy and visibility’ (Rose-Redwood et al., 2010, p. 457).” (Williamson, 2023).

Mexico’s lawsuit against Google for renaming the whole Gulf proves that people will fight and struggle for legitimacy and visibility to not be erased from the narrative. Mexico has argued that the U.S. had only renamed their shelf of the Gulf, not the whole thing, so Google should fix it to be factually correct.

By renaming and reshaping the history of a place, Google is actively colonising the Gulf of Mexico for the U.S. government.

The Cambridge dictionary defines colonization as “the act or process of filling a place or taking control of an area of activity.”

Renaming it to the Gulf of America, ownership moves to the U.S. and thus so does the history, and the history will be rewritten to show how America was never in the wrong when in fact they were. The truth of colonization will be buried along with the name Gulf of Mexico.

This brings me to my conclusion that by something as simple as renaming a place can cause an entire history to disappear and spark controversy of colonization. It also shows that not only will the history be threatened, but the culture will suffer as well, it will fight for legitimacy. History in any sense of the word is always rewritten by the winners, and the winners are usually the ones with power. Google has an abundance of power and is choosing to sit in the lap of the U.S. government.
