The World in www / JavaScript DOM

Elri Greyling

The reading I chose to do a close reading on is Ballatore et al's Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results (2017). I will format this post by showing the quote, analysing it, and then giving more information on it as that is what makes sense to me on how to present this analysis in a functional way.

"The digital augmentations of places, in other words, are beginning to matter as much as their material counter-parts, and much research has shown how they can have significant impacts on how we economically, socially, and politically interact with our environments (see Kitchin and Dodge 2011; Graham, De Sabbata, and Zook 2015). Digital code and content thus do not just reflect the world but also produce it."

This quote means that physical landmarks and buildings are becoming immortalised in the digital world, and just like one would maintain a physical building, their digital equivalent needs to keep up to date with the new version. Ballatore et. al states that there was research done by Kitchin and Dodge 2011; Graham, De Sabbata, and Zook 2015, and there are 3 different ways it impacts interaction, those being economically, socially and politically. The digital world does not just reflect the physical entity and its reputation, but it also produces another entity that is in collaboration with the physical. The digital world is making the physical have an online presence to attract more people because the people who can afford to go visit other places can also afford an internet connection. Additional research from Lama (2024) states that there are multiple reasons for businesses to engage in having an online presence, from both a tourist's and business's perspectives. There are overlapping reasons such as, destination discovering, real time information, Virtual tourism/exploration, interaction with influencers, and booking reservations.

"Because the Web has long been envisaged as a participatory tool, and because its usership now approaches 3.5 billion people (almost half of the world’s population), many have hoped that the construction of information geographies could become more open, participatory, and democratic."

I use this quote to show how far we have come since 2017. According to Petrosyan (2024), the number of internet users have increased and sits now at 5.56 billion people. That is two thirds of the world population at the time of the latest statistics. This means that the internet has grown to include more users in participating, something Ballatore had hoped.

"Elwood (2006) noted that little has changed for those at the bottom of the digital divide, and financial and skill barriers continue to influence who gets a say in digital representations of the world and who does not (see also Craig and Elwood 1998)."

Those who cannot afford to access the internet and those who do not have the skills to navigate the internet has less accurate representation. This is because the people who represent them do not necessarily know what they need. Oftentimes it is because they need other necessities more than the internet, like housing and proper water. This is a failure on the government's part, so I will not be going into that too much because it is a whole other essay topic.

"Most people use a digital search as their gateway not just to a disembodied Web but also to their lived everyday geographies: using it to learn about a destination, shop, navigate, and perform countless other activities."

Things of the internet that has replaced others are GPS, online shopping, scheduling appointments or booking a reservation, streaming services, online banking, and email.

"A user has a requirement for information, and the available set of published information is much larger than the user has utility for. Search engines thus do not just direct a user to relevant information but filter that information to distinguish between more and less useful content."

There is a lot of knowledge online and it is overwhelming to search through them all to search for the user's specific thing. The top results when you do an online search is the information people have found most relevant when typing in a similar query, or at least has the most clicks. Google (2025) actually explains their system in the following list:

"To return relevant results, we first need to establish what you’re looking for - the intent behind your query. Next, our systems analyze the content to assess whether it contains information that might be relevant to what you are looking for. We also use aggregated and anonymized interaction data to assess whether search results are relevant to queries. After identifying relevant content, our systems aim to prioritize those that seem most helpful. To do this, they identify signals that can help determine which content demonstrates expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness. Our systems also consider the usability of content. When all other signals are relatively equal, content that people will find more accessible may perform better. Search aims to connect human curiosity to knowledge as accurately as possible. To do this, we use information such as your location, past Search history, and Search settings to determine what is most relevant for you in the moment. Our systems can recognize if you have visited the same page multiple times before and bring that page to the top of your Search results. Or, if you are using the same query, we may show new perspectives and top stories from across the web."
"We computed the indicator L for the 144 countries. Because each case includes a Google query at four different times, the localness for a country is expressed as mean L of the four temporal snapshots and the corresponding standard deviation (SD). As shown in Figure 5, the value of L varies widely across the data set. Figure 5 compares the distribution of L in U.S. Google (median D 0.22) with local Google results (median D 0.37). The gap between the distributions reflects the expected fact that results for U.S. Google in English are less local than the localized versions of Google in different languages. The distribution of local Google is particularly wide, ranging from 0 to 1 without large gaps, indicating that cases exist for every level of localness."

L means localness. Basically what this means is that the researchers have compared the distribution of localness in U.S. Google with local Google results. It is shown that search results in U.S. Google in English are less local than other places using a localized version of Google in different languages. Below is an image of the results:

"Whereas some countries receive results from a few dominant countries, others receive results from a broader range of sources. To quantify diversity at the country level, we draw an ecological analogy comparing species diversity in an ecosystem with the diversity of countries of origin in the URLs. Hence, we compute a widely used indicator of diversity based on Shannon entropy (Levine and HilleRisLambers 2009) on the URLs, obtaining an index ranging between 0 (low diversity) and 2 (high diversity)."

So, while places get results from any dominant countries, it is a disadvantage to those living in the dominant countries as they do not get the diversity of other places, whereas those not living in the dominant countries get a lot more than their small local range of resources.

"Although the full ten-variable model explains a substantial amount of the variation in L, the collinearities highlighted in Table 5 make the model difficult to interpret. To provide a more interpretable model M1, we identified four variables using forward selection that explain 53 percent of the total variance: h-index, region (a categorical variable with seven World Bank regions), the percentage of Internet users, and a Boolean factor indicating whether or not English is considered a “local” language in the country by Google:"

What the formula means is the following: L means the localness, h-index means a country's level of engagement, internet users means the amount of digital infrastructure that supports the resources. The answer to the model, or the results, are shown along with the next two tables.

"To summarize, we find that geographic region, strength of scholarly publishing, Internet infrastructure, and language barriers all play important roles in shaping the geography of Google Search results. These four variables explain roughly half of the variation in L, leaving much room to evaluate the role of other country-specific attributes. We note that these explanatory variables do exhibit significant collinearities, making it difficult to neatly unpack the roles of individual factors. We also find significant differences between the explanatory models for the U.S. and local results; countries not in North America exhibit much higher L values in local results, and a country’s scholarly network is more predictive of L in local results."

"This investigation of the geography of Google Search results shows that wealthy and well-connected countries tend to have much more locally produced content that is visible about them than poor and poorly connected countries. Even cities located in countries with huge populations such as Lagos show a tendency toward having relatively little local content about them in Google Search results. This means that a user in the United States or Germany searching for cities is far more likely to be given access to locally produced content than a Tanzanian or Cambodian."

Because of the lack of resources to produce content in the local context, the poor and poorly connected countries are reliant on the information coming from the dominant countries, so they do not really have anything relating to them, whereas the wealthy and well-connected countries have more relevant information available. Population in this case does not mean the same as accessible to the internet. The next quote is an extension of my observation:

"The United States, in particular, is a dominant content-producing force, even when excluding Wikipedia, which is a highly visible U.S.-based but globally assembled resource (Figures 10 and 11). In the results for sixty-one countries, the United States supplies over half of the first page content on Google. This means that not only are U.S. Internet users surrounded by an extremely locally produced Internet, but U.S.-produced content is highly visible in much of the rest of the world. This does not necessarily mean that the United States is an informational hegemon everywhere in the world, however. France has a somewhat smaller sphere of influence, mainly limited to countries in Africa, whereas Russia produces a visible effect only on results about Kyrgyzstan (see Figure 10)."

The reading does say that they have mainly focused on capital cities and that their localness indicator did not take into account what search results people actually interact with and the multitude of the devices / media across the search engines. They state that they are not without vault and some noise might have been able to slip in undetected because it is difficult to keep track of that. Simply put, the conclusion reads that the Global North have the current monopoly on the distribution of content online because they are simply more advanced in the field than the Global South.
