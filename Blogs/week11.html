Digital Inequalities / JavaScript Events

Elri Greyling

I chose Making a Feminist Internet in Africa: Why the Internet needs African Feminists and Feminisms. I will be explaining quotes from the article that I felt was important to the message stated in its title.

"With many gatherings and meetings being cancelled and postponed, the quick and easy solution that is being shared around is “well then, let’s just meet online”, and therein lies the problem: presenting the internet as a solution to a social problem, as some kind of communication silver bullet that imagines an equality of access, use and representation on the internet which is false. And this is why we need even more African feminists and feminisms on, in, around the internet, to counter the idea that technology somehow levels the playing field for all, and is an infallible solution to all our problems."

People use the internet as a solution to meet with others, which can be useful but not everyone has equal access to use the internet so the representation of the availability is incorrect. The author's solution is that in order to level the playing field for everyone, more African feminists need to be involved in and around the internet to go against the idea that technology is a one-size-fits-all solution.

"When the All Women Count-Take Back The Tech! (AWC-TBTT!) project at the Association for Progressive Communications Women’s Rights Programme (APC WRP) decided to host a Making a Feminist Internet in Africa and the Diaspora (MFIAfrica) gathering in October 2019, the goal was clear. Find and bring together as diverse as possible a group of African activists, thinkers, artists and feminists to talk about technology, the internet and the power and impact it has on our lives and our work. This was not the first gathering of its kind, but it was the first such gathering held in Africa, with and for African feminists and activists."

The meeting invited a diverse group of people from Africa: activists, thinkers, artists, feminists. They were to talk about how technology and internet has a power over them and the impact it has on their lives and work. This was the first of these gatherings that happened in Africa, by Africans for Africans.

"Technology has thrust communities of people online and offline into a space where the online realities and emerging issues do not have offline processes and recourse. Online gender-based violence, for example, has been a pressing concern raised by many women, LGBTIQA and gender non-conforming people, but legislation that recognises and acts on these attacks and violence is largely absent in many African countries. Because of this, the online space has become a “safe space” for people and communities of people to attack women, LGBTIQA people and enbies and mostly get away with it."

Communities had been forced to go online as well as online and this has caused some of the communities to not have a system in place for when it goes offline. Things like gender-based violence have been causing problems before the internet, but with the internet, that hate is spread faster and further. The people who can push the law onto the cyberbullies in reality are absent in Africa. And because of this, the cyberbullies get away with targeting their victims both online and offline.

"The inequality of access to and representation on the internet is fast replicating existing oppression and suppression."

The imbalance of people that have access to the internet and representation on the internet to the people without it is quickly causing the digital divide to widen and duplicates the current oppression and suppression, so the divide alienates those who do not have access or representation on the internet.

"In the dark corner of the popular narratives of Africa and its people (and problems) you will find shelved the stories of women, queers, and people with disabilities."

History is written by the winners, and all the history of the losers are buried into dark corners where it is near impossible to find. In this case, the narratives of Africans that you do not hear is also buried away. Those stories are by women, queers, and people with disabilities.

"The internet and technology are a powerful space, I daresay force, that African women and LGBTIQA people have always contributed to. Our everyday lives extend to online platforms and tools where we plan and share and love and grow. The internet has helped shape how we are perceived and how too we perceive ourselves. The internet is also a site of great violence that is directed at people who express themselves in ways and voices that do not conform."

The internet and technology had been shaped by many, INCLUDING African women and LGBTQIA+ people. In return, the internet and technology had also shaped the way we are seen and how we see ourselves. Unfortunately, the internet was also shaped by bullies who do nothing but tear us down for who we are.

"Without a feminist internet that ensures that all people can access and influence content and governance of this internet, we will be replicating a violent and oppressive way of life that might eventually be inescapable."

Without the feminine, the toxic masculine (I'm not saying all masculine, I'm saying the toxic part of it) will keep and take over the internet. The few good masculine will be erased if they do not have the backing of millions upon millions of femininity.

To quote Chappell Roan, "What we really need is a femininomenon!" (Feminine phenomenon)
